Introduction #

Scottish philosopher David Hume’s essay Of the balance of trade: the first real exposition of an economic model. Published in 1758, almost 20 years before Adam Smith’s The Wealth of Nations. The gap between imports and exports is an indicator of growing international linkages. What is International Trade? - the subject matter of international economics consists of issues raised by the special problems of economic interactions between sovereign (a state with a defined territory) states.

Gains from Trade #

The most important single insight in all of international economics is that there are gains from trade, i.e. when countries sell goods and services to each other this exchange is almost always to their mutual benefit.

Pattern of Trade #

Economists cannot discuss the effect of international trade or recommend changes if they know their theory is good enough to explain the international trade that is currently observed in the real world. Thus attempts to explain the pattern of international trade have been a major pre-occupation of international economists. Some aspects of the pattern of trade are easy to understand. Climate and resources clearly explain why Brazil exports coffee beans and Saudi Arabia exports oil. Still, most of the patterns are more subtle. In the early nineteenth century David Ricardo offered an explanation of trade in terms of international differences in labour productivity.

Labour Productivity and Comparative Advantage: The Ricardian Model #

Countries engage in international trade for two basic reasons, first, because they are different and hence can benefit from their differences by reaching an arrangement in which each does the things it does relatively well. Second, to achieve economies of scale in production, i.e if each country produces only a limited range of goods, then it can produce at a much larger scale and hence more efficiently than if it tried to produce everything.

The concept of Comparative Advantage #

Opportunity costs are trade-offs that must be bourne in order to allocate resources to the production of one good, as there are many goods that can be produced with the resources. The difference in opportunity costs offers the possibility of a mutually beneficial rearrangement of world production/ international trade. International Trade produces increase in the world output by allowing each country to specialize in producing the good in which it has a comparative advantage. A country has a comparative advantage in producing a good if the opportunity cost of producing that good in terms of the other good is lower in that country than it is in the other country/countries.

To answer further questions we must be much more explicit in our analysis. In this chapter we will develop a model of international trade originally developed by the British economist David Ricardo, who introduced the concept of comparative advantage in the early nineteenth century. This approach is known as the Ricardian model.

One Factor Economy #

To begin the study of comparative advantage in determining the pattern of international trade, we begin by imagining that we are dealing with an economy - which we call Home - that has only one factor of production. We also imagine that only two goods, wine and cheese are produced. The technology of Home’s economy can be summarised by labour productivity in each industry expressed in terms of the unit labour requirement. the number of hours of labour required to produce one pound of cheese or a gallon of wine. We define \(a_{LW}\) and \(a_{LC}\) as the unit labour requirements in wine and cheese production, respectively. The economy’s total resources are defined as \(L\), the total labour supply.

Production Possibilities #

Since any economy has limited resources, there are limits on what it can produce, and there are always trade-offs; to produce more of one good the economy must sacrifice some production of another good. The trade-offs are illustrated graphically by a production possibility frontier, which shows the maximum amount of wine that can be produced once the decision has been made to produce any given amount of cheese and vice versa. When there is only one factor of production, the production possibility frontier of an economy is simply a straight line1: If \(Q_W\) is the economy’s production of wine and \(Q_C\) is the production of cheese, then the labour used in producing wine will be \(a_{LW}Q_W\), similarly the labour used in producing cheese will be \(a_{LC}Q_C\). The production possibility frontier is determined by the limits on the economy’s resources – in this case, labour. Because the economy’s total labour supply is \(L\), the limits on production are defined by the inequality:

$$a_{LC}Q_{C}+a_{LW}Q_W \leq L$$

When the production possibility curve is a straight line, the opportunity cost of a pound of cheese in terms of wine is constant, i.e. to produce another one pound of cheese would require \(a_{LC}\) person hours, each of these person-hours could in turn have been used to produce \(1/a_{LW}\) gallons of wine, thus the opportunity cost of cheese in terms of wine is \(a_{LC}/a_{LW}\). This opportunity cost is the absolute value of the slope of the production possibility frontier.
